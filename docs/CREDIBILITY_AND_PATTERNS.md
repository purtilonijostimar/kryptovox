# Kryptovox — Credibility Scoring & Pattern Recognition
**Version 0.1 | March 2026**

---

## The Ground Truth Problem

We cannot verify that any encounter occurred. Therefore a credibility score cannot mean "we believe this witness."

It must mean something more precise:

> **"This testimony has the structural, linguistic, and emotional characteristics of genuine experiential memory rather than fabricated or confabulated narrative."**

This is a distinction forensic psychology has studied extensively. We apply those validated tools here.

The score tells us: *how consistent is this account with how real traumatic memories are structured and recalled?*

---

## The Witness Credibility Score (WCS)

Six independent dimensions. Each scored 0–10. Weighted composite produces WCS (0–100).

---

### Dimension 1 — Internal Consistency (IC)
**Weight: 20%**

Does the account hold together with itself? Fabricated stories tend to either be too clean (over-rehearsed, no contradictions, perfectly linear) or break down under their own weight (timeline inconsistencies, physical impossibilities).

**What we measure:**
- Timeline coherence: does the sequence of events hold up?
- Physical consistency: does the creature description stay stable throughout, or does it drift?
- Distance/time plausibility: do stated distances, durations, and movements make physical sense?
- Self-contradiction count: how many times does the witness contradict something they said earlier?

**Scoring rubric:**
| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 9–10 | No contradictions. Timeline coherent. Physical details stable. Minor uncertainties acknowledged. |
| 7–8 | One minor inconsistency, likely memory artifact. Core account stable. |
| 5–6 | 2–3 inconsistencies. Some drift in description. Plausible as imperfect memory. |
| 3–4 | Multiple inconsistencies. Significant description drift. Timeline gaps. |
| 0–2 | Major contradictions. Timeline impossible. Description fundamentally changes. |

**Important distinction:** 
Genuine memory has *peripheral* uncertainty (witness is unsure about details they weren't focused on) but *core* stability (the central experience stays consistent). Fabricated accounts often show the opposite — confident about peripheral details (over-constructed) but inconsistent about what should be the most memorable moments.

---

### Dimension 2 — Sensory Specificity (SS)
**Weight: 20%**

Real memories encode sensory experience. Fabricated accounts describe what the storyteller *imagines* something would look like — which is primarily visual and generic.

**Based on:** Reality Monitoring framework (Johnson & Raye, 1981) — validated method for distinguishing real memories from imagined ones. Real memories contain significantly more perceptual detail across multiple sensory channels.

**What we measure:**

*Visual detail (count unique visual specifics beyond basic description):*
- Colour gradients, texture, surface quality, movement quality, posture
- "The fur looked matted and wet in patches" vs "it had brown fur"

*Auditory detail:*
- Specific sound descriptions ("like a dog crossed with something deeper, a resonance you felt in your chest" vs "it growled")

*Olfactory detail:*
- Specific smell descriptions (musk, rot, sulphur, wet dog, iron — and combinations)

*Tactile/thermal:*
- Temperature, air movement, physical sensations the witness experienced

*Kinesthetic:*
- Their own body responses — legs went cold, couldn't move, heart pounding, voice wouldn't work

*Peripheral details:*
- What they were doing immediately before ("I was about to light a cigarette")
- What they were thinking about ("I was annoyed because my boot had come unlaced")
- Environmental context (weather, sounds of normal wildlife before and after, light quality)

**Scoring rubric:**
| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 9–10 | Rich multi-sensory description across ≥4 sensory channels. Strong peripheral detail. Specific texture/quality language. |
| 7–8 | Good detail across 3 channels. Some peripheral detail. Specific language in at least one channel. |
| 5–6 | Primarily visual. Some specificity. Minimal peripheral detail. |
| 3–4 | Generic description. One or two sensory channels. No peripheral context. |
| 0–2 | Bare visual description only. No sensory texture. No peripheral detail. |

---

### Dimension 3 — Spontaneity & Authenticity Markers (SA)
**Weight: 15%**

Genuine witnesses volunteer details unprompted, correct themselves, admit uncertainty, and show self-awareness that their account is unbelievable. Fabricators tend to tell a constructed story with less self-interruption.

**Based on:** CBCA criteria 3 (Unstructured production), 12 (Spontaneous corrections), 13 (Admitting lack of memory), 14 (Raising doubts about own testimony)

**What we measure:**

*Unprompted details:*
Count of specific details volunteered without interviewer question. "Oh, and I remember — its hands. I didn't mention its hands. The fingers were longer than they should be and they were dark at the tips."

*Self-corrections:*
Witness corrects themselves mid-account without being challenged. "It was about 8 feet — no wait, I need to think about this. I'm 6 foot and it was at least a head and a half taller than me, so maybe closer to 7 and a half."

*Uncertainty admissions:*
"I can't tell you exactly what colour its eyes were because I couldn't look directly at them for long." This is the opposite of fabrication — fabricators tend to be too certain.

*Self-deprecating credibility flags:*
The "I know how this sounds" family of statements. "I'm not a person who believes in this stuff." "I've never told anyone because I knew they'd think I was crazy." These are strong authenticity markers — fabricators seeking attention don't typically undermine their own story.

*Non-linear account structure:*
Real memories are not linear. Witnesses loop back, jump to associated details, correct chronology. "Wait, I need to back up — before this happened, there was something that I now think was leading up to it..."

**Scoring rubric:**
| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 9–10 | Multiple unprompted details. Self-corrections present. Uncertainty admissions. Strong self-deprecating credibility flags. Non-linear structure. |
| 7–8 | Some unprompted details. At least one self-correction or uncertainty admission. Some non-linearity. |
| 5–6 | Mostly responds to questions. Some spontaneous detail. Mainly linear. |
| 3–4 | Entirely reactive to questions. No self-correction. Perfect linear narrative. |
| 0–2 | Scripted-feeling. Resists all uncertainty. No spontaneous additions. |

---

### Dimension 4 — Emotional Authenticity (EA)
**Weight: 25%**

This is the highest-weighted dimension because it is the hardest to fake at scale and the most studied.

**Based on:** Trauma testimony research, PTSD verbal markers, CBCA emotional content criteria, voice analysis research.

**What we measure:**

*Emotional break events — are they narratively appropriate?*
The key test: does the witness break down at the moment in the story that *warrants* breaking down? A witness who cries when describing the creature making eye contact — a frozen moment of direct confrontation — is showing a response pattern consistent with genuine traumatic recall. A witness who cries arbitrarily, or exclusively at narrative peaks they've clearly rehearsed, is not.

Tagged by: moment in narrative, emotional type (voice break, crying, long pause, inability to continue), what immediately preceded it.

*Post-encounter life impact:*
Did the experience change their behaviour? Genuine trauma tends to:
- Change relationship with the environment (stopped hunting alone, won't go back to that area)
- Create intrusive thoughts ("I still can't stop thinking about its eyes")
- Affect sleep
- Create reluctance to share (years of silence, told spouse/close friend only)

*Disclosure pattern:*
When did they first tell anyone? Who? How did people respond? The typical Kryptovox pattern — years of silence, eventual disclosure after unbearable isolation of the experience, fear of ridicule — is consistent with genuine anomalous experience. It is inconsistent with someone fabricating for attention, who would tell immediately.

*Voice quality markers (from audio):*
- Rate changes (slowing at key moments — real memory retrieval takes time)
- Volume drops (witnesses often speak quieter when describing the most intense moments)
- Breath patterns
- Throat clearing or swallowing before difficult sections

*Physiological description:*
Witnesses describing their own body's response — tunnel vision, time distortion, involuntary paralysis, inability to scream — these are documented acute stress responses. Fabricators rarely know these in sufficient detail to reproduce them accurately.

**Scoring rubric:**
| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 9–10 | Emotional breaks at narratively appropriate moments. Post-encounter life impact described. Long disclosure delay. Physiological stress responses described accurately. Voice quality markers present. |
| 7–8 | Some emotional breaks with clear narrative triggers. Some post-impact described. Meaningful disclosure delay. |
| 5–6 | Emotional content present but without strong narrative anchoring. Some impact. |
| 3–4 | Emotional display without clear triggers. Little post-impact. Quick disclosure. |
| 0–2 | Flat emotional affect throughout OR emotional display at every point equally (performing emotion rather than experiencing recollection). |

---

### Dimension 5 — Context Plausibility (CP)
**Weight: 10%**

Does the context of the encounter make basic sense? Is the witness a plausible witness in a plausible location for a plausible reason?

**What we measure:**
- Witness had a legitimate reason to be in that location at that time
- Terrain matches stated activity (hunting, hiking, driving)
- Geographic details are internally consistent and consistent with stated location
- Time of day is appropriate to stated activity
- The witness's prior experience level (experienced outdoorsperson vs urban person in unfamiliar woods) is consistent with their description of the environment

**Note:** This dimension is weighted lowest because it can be gamed most easily by a fabricator who puts basic research in. It also penalises urban witnesses unfairly. Use as a tiebreaker only.

---

### Dimension 6 — Corroboration (CO)
**Weight: 10%**

Any external support for the account, however indirect.

**What we measure:**
- Multiple witnesses present: each independently described the encounter similarly
- Physical evidence mentioned: tracks found, damage to property, dead animals nearby
- Prior reports from same area: other independent accounts from same location
- Reported to authorities: filed with police, park service, etc. (high cost action — people who are lying for attention rarely incur the social cost of official reports)
- Behavioural corroboration: others noted the witness's distress immediately after (spouse, friend — not in the account but mentioned)

**Scoring rubric:**
| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 8–10 | Multiple witnesses OR physical evidence AND prior area reports |
| 5–7 | One corroborating factor (other witness OR evidence OR prior reports) |
| 3–4 | Reported to one credible person immediately after |
| 0–2 | Entirely uncorroborated solo account |

## Composite WCS Calculation

```
WCS = (IC × 0.20) + (SS × 0.20) + (SA × 0.15) + (EA × 0.25) + (CP × 0.10) + (CO × 0.10)
```

Scale: 0–10 (multiply by 10 for 0–100 display)

### Interpretation Bands

| WCS | Label | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 8.5–10 | **Exceptional** | Testimony consistent with genuine traumatic memory in all major dimensions. |
| 7.0–8.4 | **Strong** | Testimony shows strong authenticity markers with minor gaps. |
| 5.5–6.9 | **Moderate** | Testimony has some authenticity markers but also notable weaknesses. |
| 4.0–5.4 | **Weak** | Testimony shows limited authenticity markers. Fabrication not ruled out. |
| 0–3.9 | **Unreliable** | Testimony shows patterns inconsistent with genuine memory. Excluded from statistical analysis (kept in database with flag). |

### Validation

The WCS is validated by:
1. Applying it to confirmed hoaxes → these must score significantly lower
2. Applying it to fictional accounts → these must score significantly lower
3. Applying it to documented genuine wildlife trauma accounts → these must score comparably to high-WCS Kryptovox accounts
4. Inter-rater reliability: two independent scorers on 10% of accounts, κ ≥ 0.75 target

## Pattern Recognition System

The WCS tells us how much to trust an account. The Pattern Recognition System tells us what the accounts collectively describe.

Four layers of pattern detection running simultaneously — each catches things the others miss.

---

### Layer A — Structured Descriptor Analysis

The extraction schema produces a feature vector for each account. Every physical and behavioural descriptor is a variable.

**Methods:**

*Descriptor frequency analysis*
Baseline: how often does each descriptor appear across all accounts?
The self-illuminated eye descriptor: what % of accounts include it? Is that rate different between Dogman and Bigfoot accounts?

*Co-occurrence analysis*
Which descriptors always appear together? Which are mutually exclusive?
Example hypothesis: self-illuminated eyes co-occur with sulphur/musk smell at above-chance rates.
Method: phi coefficient matrix across all binary descriptors.

*Cluster analysis*
Are there distinct subtypes of encounter/creature within each phenomenon?
Method: hierarchical clustering on descriptor vectors. Examine whether accounts group into meaningful subtypes (e.g., "tall dark silent type" vs "aggressive howler").

*Discriminant analysis: Dogman vs Bigfoot*
Which descriptors most reliably distinguish the two phenomena?
Method: Linear discriminant analysis on descriptor vectors, Dogman vs Bigfoot as class labels.

---

### Layer B — Semantic Embedding Analysis

The structured schema can only capture what we thought to ask about. The embedding layer catches everything else.

**Method:**

Each transcript (or meaningful segment: physical description, encounter moment, departure) is embedded using a language model (OpenAI text-embedding-3-large or equivalent).

These embeddings are stored in Supabase (pgvector extension).

**What this enables:**

*Cross-account similarity search*
"Find me the 20 accounts most similar to this one semantically." Surface clusters of accounts that use nearly identical language without having been coded with identical labels.

*Anomalous detail detection*
Find passages that appear in multiple accounts but were *not* captured in the structured schema — unexpected recurring language that the schema missed.

*Historical record matching*
Embed both modern accounts and historical records. Find semantic similarity between a 2019 Michigan account and a medieval European werewolf trial transcript. Similarity above threshold → flag for human review.

*The "specific unusual phrase" test*
Certain specific phrases appear in testimony that are both unusual and not media-contaminated. The "self-illuminated" eye description. The specific paralysis description. The "it just watched me" departure pattern. Search the embedding space for clusters around these anchor phrases.

---

### Layer C — Temporal and Narrative Structure Analysis

When in the account does each type of information appear? Is there a consistent narrative structure across accounts?

**What we measure:**

*Information introduction timing*
At what point in the account (as % of total length) does the witness introduce: the first anomalous detail / the creature's eyes / the emotional peak / the resolution?

Fabricated stories tend to have artificial narrative structure (setup → rising action → climax → resolution). Real memories have stranger timing — key details emerge late, peripheral details appear early.

*Emotional break mapping*
For each account where emotional breaks are tagged with timestamps: at what narrative moment does each break type occur?

Expected finding: voice breaks should cluster at eye-contact moments and vocalization moments. Crying should cluster at departure moments (the creature leaving — which witnesses often describe as somehow the most disturbing part).

If emotional breaks cluster at consistent narrative moments across hundreds of independent accounts, that is not something that can be fabricated at scale.

*Disclosure timeline*
How long between encounter and first disclosure? First public disclosure? The distribution of this variable across the corpus.

---

### Layer D — Geographic and Environmental Pattern Analysis

**Methods:**

*Kernel density estimation*
Plot all accounts on a map. Where do clusters form? Are clusters random with respect to population density, or do they suggest terrain preference?

*Terrain covariate analysis*
For each account location: extract terrain type (forest/grassland/wetland), elevation, distance to nearest water body, human population density within 10km, distance to nearest town.

Regression: which terrain variables predict encounter density?

*Dogman vs Bigfoot territory*
Do the two phenomena occupy distinct geographic ranges or overlap significantly?

*Historical overlap*
Do modern hotspots match locations mentioned in historical accounts or associated with folkloric entities?

*The Slovenian map*
Within Slovenia: map the locations associated with relevant folkloric entities. What terrain? What elevation? What historical land use? How does this compare to the modern international hotspot profile?

### Pattern Confidence Scoring

Not all patterns are equal. Each identified pattern gets a Pattern Confidence Score (PCS):

```
PCS = (frequency_score × 0.30) +
      (cross_cultural_independence × 0.25) +
      (pre_media_presence × 0.20) +
      (specificity_score × 0.15) +
      (schema_independent_confirmation × 0.10)
```

**frequency_score:** How often does this pattern appear in the corpus? (normalised 0–1)

**cross_cultural_independence:** Does this pattern appear across accounts from different countries/regions with no plausible cultural contact?

**pre_media_presence:** Does this pattern appear in pre-internet-era accounts (collected before 2000) at similar rates to post-internet accounts? If yes, less likely to be media contamination.

**specificity_score:** Is this pattern a *specific* descriptor not predicted by generic folklore templates (high score) or a generic one any scary creature story might include (low score)?

**schema_independent_confirmation:** Was this pattern identified by the embedding layer *before* being added to the structured schema? Independent identification by two different methods is stronger evidence.

### PCS Interpretation

| PCS | Label |
|---|---|
| 0.80–1.00 | **Core pattern** — appears in final paper as primary finding |
| 0.60–0.79 | **Supporting pattern** — reported with confidence interval caveats |
| 0.40–0.59 | **Emerging pattern** — noted, requires more data |
| 0.00–0.39 | **Noise** — insufficient evidence, not reported |

## The Self-Illuminated Eye Hypothesis — A Worked Example

This single descriptor illustrates the full system:

**Structured extraction:**
Every account coded: eyes_glow (Y/N), eyes_colour, eyes_described_as_self_illuminated (Y/N — distinct from simply reflective).

**Frequency analysis:**
What % of Dogman accounts include self-illuminated eyes? What % of Bigfoot accounts? Are these rates different?

**Co-occurrence:**
Does self-illuminated eyes co-occur with specific colours (amber, orange, red) more than others? Does it co-occur with other descriptors (sulphur smell, tall, approach-then-held-still)?

**Pre-media test:**
The self-illuminated eye descriptor is *not* standard in popular Bigfoot/Dogman media. Films and TV typically show reflective eyes (like an animal) not self-illuminated. If pre-2000 accounts show this descriptor at similar rates to post-2000, it is unlikely to be media-contaminated.

**Historical record:**
Does self-illuminated / light-producing eyes appear in the historical records? Anubis is depicted with eyes that glow in Egyptian art. The Cynocephali in Pliny have "eyes that shine in the dark." Medieval werewolf accounts — "his eyes glowed as with an inner fire." These are the matches we look for.

**Embedding search:**
Semantic search across all transcripts for clusters around the phrase. Find every passage where a witness describes the eyes in unusual detail. Surface patterns within those passages that the structured schema missed.

**PCS calculation:**
If self-illuminated eyes appears in >40% of Dogman accounts, appears pre-media at similar rates, appears in historically isolated cultural records, and is confirmed by both structured extraction AND embedding cluster analysis — it scores a Core Pattern with PCS >0.80.

That is a finding. That goes in the paper.

## The Honest Limitation

The WCS and PCS systems are only as good as the calibration.

Without ground truth — without confirmed real encounters — we cannot directly validate that a high WCS means an encounter occurred. What we can validate is that high-WCS accounts are structurally different from confirmed hoaxes and fiction.

That is the claim we make. No more.

The patterns that emerge from the corpus tell us what these accounts collectively describe. Whether what they describe exists is a question the data cannot answer. It can only make that question harder to dismiss.
