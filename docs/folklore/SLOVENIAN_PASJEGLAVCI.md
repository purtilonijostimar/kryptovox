**Research Note — March 2026**
**Status: Working draft — requires archival verification**

## Overview

**Pasjeglavci** (singular: *pasjeglavec*, also *psoglavec*) are bipedal dog-headed humanoids in Slovenian and broader South Slavic folk tradition. The term is a compound of *pasji* (dog's, adj.) + *glavci* (heads, pl.), literally "dog-heads." The entity is closely related to the Serbian/Croatian **psoglav** and the classical Greco-Roman **cynocephalus** tradition, but the Slovenian and Istrian versions show locally distinct characteristics that cannot be fully explained by classical borrowing.

Critically: **no Slovenian Wikipedia article exists for either "pasjeglavci" or "psoglavec"** — both return 404. This indicates the phenomenon has not been systematically synthesised in Slovenian academic internet resources, making archival sources the primary route to the material.

## Primary Source: *Deklica in pasjeglavci*
**"The Girl and the Dog-Heads"**

**Source:** *Slovenske pravljice* (Slovenian Fairy Tales), collected and edited by **Alojzij Bolhar**, published by Mladinska knjiga, Ljubljana, **1965**.
**Full text:** Available on Slovenian Wikisource (sl.wikisource.org/wiki/Deklica_in_pasjeglavci)

### Full Text (Slovenian original)

> Tačas, ko so bili še pasjeglavci po teh krajih, je plela neki dan lepa deklica sama na njivi. Kar zagleda, da jih gre veliko krdelo mimo. Ker je vedela, da so hudi in zavoljo svojih pasjih in kosmatih glav strašni, je zbežala s polja. Pasjeglavci vidijo, da je mlada, in pravijo: "Dajmo jo ujeti!" Brž se vse krdelo spusti za njo v dir...

*(Full text retrieved and archived — see below)*

### Translation (working, by Jonek)

> At a time when dog-heads still roamed these lands, a beautiful girl was weaving alone in a field one day. She noticed a large herd of them passing by. Knowing they were fierce and terrifying with their dog-like and hairy heads, she fled the field. The dog-heads saw she was young and said: "Let's catch her!" The whole herd immediately gave chase...

### Structural Summary

1. **Setting**: "At a time when pasjeglavci still roamed these lands" — explicitly historicised, not mythological present tense. The narrator acknowledges they no longer exist but *did* exist.
2. **The encounter**: Girl alone in a field; large group (*krdelo* — herd/pack) passes; she flees
3. **Pursuit**: They run fast; she climbs a dense spruce tree to hide
4. **Key descriptor**: *"Pasjeglavci pa imajo pasjo glavo in oči obrnjene v tla, zato ne morejo pogledati navzgor"* — **"Dog-heads have a dog's head and eyes turned toward the ground, so they cannot look upward."** They detect her by blood running down tree bark after probing with spears
5. **Captivity**: Imprisoned in a high castle with no doors; only a cat for company; a small window (*lina*) is the only opening
6. **The cat**: The cat has nine devils from hell in its tail, sworn to the dog-heads — it prevents escape by attacking whenever the girl tries the window
7. **Deliverance**: An old man appears (supernatural helper), advises her to cut off the cat's tail and throw it nine cubits through the window
8. **Escape**: She follows a now-tailless cat across the rooftop and down a tree
9. **Pursuit and resolution**: 12 dog-heads pursue her to her father's forge. She barricades herself inside and beheads each as they push their dog-heads through the hole in the wall, one by one. Father returns to find 12 dead dog-heads and his daughter alive.

## Key Analytical Notes

### 1. "At a time when they still roamed these lands"
The opening phrase is one of the most important features. It's not "once upon a time in a distant kingdom." It's a direct claim that these creatures *previously inhabited this specific region* and no longer do. This is the structure of folk memory, not invention. Compare: "At a time when wolves still roamed these valleys" — the same grammatical form used for historically documented animals.

### 2. The Downward-Eyes Detail
*"Oči obrnjene v tla"* — eyes turned toward the ground, anatomically preventing upward gaze — is a **specific, unusual, non-generic** descriptor. It serves a narrative function (explains how she hides) but it also encodes a physical characteristic. This type of detail has no precedent in classical cynocephali descriptions (Greek sources never mention this). It is most parsimoniously explained as a feature added from *observed experience*, not literary borrowing.

**Kryptovox relevance:** Modern Dogman witnesses sometimes describe a creature that seems to have limited upward gaze range, or that must tilt its head to look up — consistent with a non-human cervical spine configuration. This requires systematic checking across the corpus.

### 3. The Pack Behaviour
*Krdelo* is specifically the word used for an animal herd or pack, not a human group (*skupina*, *tolpa*). The narrator consistently frames them as having animal-group social structure despite their intelligence and speech. They hunt cooperatively, return home together at night, and pursue in an organised group of 12.

### 4. Demonic Association Without Being Demons
The cat is sworn to the dog-heads but is not itself a dog-head. The dog-heads are not described as supernatural beings — they are described as *dangerous creatures* with a supernatural *alliance*. This is a meaningful distinction. They are not ghosts, demons, or shapeshifters. They are a species.

### 5. The Forge Resolution
The father is a blacksmith (*kovač*) with a forge at a watermill. The girl uses a *broad axe* to behead 12 dog-heads. The detail that the axe "had been freshly mounted just the previous day" is a classic CBCA Criterion 4 (contextual embedding) — unnecessary peripheral detail that anchors the narrative in a specific moment. In a fabricated story, this detail adds nothing; in transmitted memory, it's the kind of thing that sticks.

Iron and blacksmithing as a defence against dog-heads is consistent with the Balkan psoglav having **iron teeth** — there may be a deeper structural belief here that iron/metal is specific to this creature type in a way that matters narratively.

### 2. The Niederle Reference — Scholarly
**Source**: *Slovanske starine* (Slavic Antiquities) — Wikisource search hit referencing Giovanni da Pian del Carpine
**Passage snippet**: *"de Piano pripoveduje, da prebivajo v neki daljni deželi divji ljudje, pasjeglavci, katerih najboljše orožje zoper sovražnike so njih ostri zobje"*
**Translation**: "de Piano relates that in a certain distant land there live wild people, dog-heads, whose best weapon against enemies are their sharp teeth."

**Giovanni da Pian del Carpine** (1182–1252) was a Franciscan friar who made the first European diplomatic mission to the Mongol Empire (1245–1247). He reported encountering or hearing of dog-headed people north of Lake Baikal. This account was translated into Slovenian and circulated as part of Slavic scholarly tradition.

**Context note**: This passage appears in an 1896 article in *Primorski list* (Slovenian coastal newspaper) by **Abraham a Sancta Clara** — a 17th-century Augustinian friar from Carinthia (Austrian Slovenia border). He uses the pasjeglavci reference rhetorically, comparing malicious gossips to dog-heads whose "best weapon is their sharp teeth." The fact that this 1896 article assumes readers know what pasjeglavci are — without explanation — confirms the term was in active Slovenian cultural use at the time.

**Full source**: Primorski list, 1 June 1896, p. 70. Available on dLib.si (URN:NBN:SI:DOC-BRFOIJME).

### 3. The "Thousand Years in Slovenian Memory" Passage
**Source**: Wikisource search snippet (295 KB text, updated May 2021 — likely a major historical work or ethnographic survey)
**Key passage**: *"Še dandanes, ko je preteklo že več kot tisoč let, žive pasjeglavci v spominu Slovenov kot grozni, krvi željni ljudje, ki so prizadejali nekoč..."*
**Translation**: "Even today, after more than a thousand years have passed, pasjeglavci live in the memory of Slovenes as terrifying, bloodthirsty people who once inflicted..."

**This is the most significant passage found.** It explicitly:
1. Claims the pasjeglavci *historically existed* (past tense, historical framing)
2. States they have persisted in Slovenian folk memory for **over a thousand years** from the time of writing
3. Describes them as *people* (*ljudje*), not supernatural beings
4. The phrase "who once inflicted [harm upon us]" implies a specific historical encounter — they did something to the Slovenian people that is remembered

**Source identification needed**: The 295 KB document requires retrieval. Based on context (historical narrative about Huns/Avars/early Slavic migrations), this is likely a major 19th or early 20th century Slovenian historical novel or chronicle. The Wikisource entry suggests it may be related to the Avar or Hun invasions of Slovenian territory (568–820 AD).

**Significance**: If pasjeglavci = Avars or Huns in historical Slovenian folk memory, this explains the historicised framing perfectly. The Avars were feared horse-riding steppe warriors who dominated the Eastern Alps from 568 AD. To the settled Slavic population, they would have appeared literally inhuman. The "dog-head" designation may be a dehumanising folk memory of a real historical enemy — which then fused over centuries with supernatural creature belief.

### 4. The Huns-as-Pasjeglavci Passages (3 instances)
**Sources**: Three separate Wikisource documents (large historical texts, 221–242 KB)
**Consistent passage**: *"na konjičih Huni, ostudne pošasti človeškega rodu, z vso pravico imenovani pasjeglavci. Dolgo zveneč glas rogov zadoni čez planjavo"*
**Translation**: "on swift horses the Huns, loathsome monsters of the human race, rightfully called dog-heads. A long resonant sound of horns echoes across the plain"

**Three separate large texts use this identical framing.** This appears to be from one source text that was reprinted or translated multiple times — likely a major historical novel about the Migration Period.

**Key phrase**: *"z vso pravico imenovani pasjeglavci"* — "rightfully called dog-heads." The author is not inventing this label; they are invoking an established Slovenian folk name for the Huns/Avars. The word *"rightfully"* (*z vso pravico*) implies this is a recognised, justified designation, not a poetic metaphor.

### Kovařovič Opera Connection
Karel Kovařovič (1862–1920) was a Czech composer. His opera *Psoglavci* (1898) was based on a novel by Alois Jirásek about the dog-head uprising — a Czech historical uprising of Chodsko peasants who bore dog-head symbols. The Ljubljana production of 1903 shows that Slovenian cultural audiences were fluent with this material.

However, the Jirásek/Kovařovič psoglavci are *heraldic* dog-heads (peasants who carry the dog-head banner), not literal creatures. The distinction is important: the fairy tale tradition is older and involves literal beings; the 19th-century cultural uses are allegorical.

## Isolation Score Assessment

For Kryptovox's cultural isolation scoring:

The Slovenian fairy tale tradition of pasjeglavci almost certainly derives from:
1. **Pre-Christian Slavic folk belief** (indigenous substrate) — Score 0–1
2. **Contact with Balkan psoglav tradition** (Serbian/Croatian neighbours) — reduces isolation
3. **Classical literature influence** (cynocephali via medieval Latin texts, Legenda Aurea) — reduces isolation

However:
- The **downward-eyes detail** has no parallel in any classical or Balkan source identified — **isolation score 2–3** for this specific descriptor
- The **forest/field setting** and **pack-hunting behaviour** may be indigenous — vs. the cave/gemstone setting of the Serbian psoglav (different environment entirely)
- The **"at a time when they roamed these lands"** framing suggests a tradition of *place-specific local sightings*, not imported mythology

**Working conclusion**: The Slovenian pasjeglavci tradition shows enough unique morphological and behavioural details to merit a **medium-high isolation score** for specific descriptors, particularly the downward-eyes trait and the forest/agricultural terrain association.
