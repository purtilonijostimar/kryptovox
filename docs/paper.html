Persistent Descriptors in Anomalous Encounter Testimony: A Forensic Credibility Analysis of the Dogman Encounter Corpus
A systematic application of structured credibility assessment to large-scale witness interview archives
Kryptovox Research Project Primary corpus: Dogman Encounters (Vic Cundiff, 2014–present) Pipeline version: 1.0 Status: Data collection in progress
Abstract

This paper presents the first systematic forensic credibility analysis of anomalous bipedal encounter testimony at corpus scale. Drawing on a primary corpus of [N] witness interviews from the Dogman Encounters podcast archive, we apply a six-dimension Witness Credibility Score (WCS) derived from established forensic psychology tools to assess the structural authenticity of individual accounts. We further conduct frequency analysis of specific anatomical and behavioural descriptors across the corpus, testing whether unusual details cluster in high-credibility accounts and whether their prevalence is stable across accounts predating widespread internet discussion of the phenomenon. We report [X]% of accounts scoring at or above the Strong credibility threshold (WCS ≥ 7.0), and identify [Y] descriptors appearing at statistically significant rates among high-credibility accounts. We discuss these findings in relation to conventional explanations — misidentification, fabrication, and social contagion — and conclude that the data presents empirical challenges that neither explanation fully addresses. We make no claim regarding the ontological status of the phenomenon described.

1. Introduction

Across North America, thousands of individuals report encounters with a large bipedal creature bearing canine facial features. These accounts are not new — analogous descriptions appear in indigenous oral tradition, colonial-era settler records, and the folklore of culturally isolated populations across multiple continents. What is new is the scale and accessibility of the testimony archive available for analysis.

Prior to the emergence of long-form podcast platforms, no comparable body of primary anomalous encounter testimony existed in accessible form. The Dogman Encounters archive alone represents over 600 unedited witness interviews conducted by a single interviewer over more than a decade — a dataset of a consistency and scale that was simply unavailable to researchers even fifteen years ago. This study represents the first attempt to apply systematic forensic credibility analysis to this archive at corpus scale.

The central question we address is not whether the described creature exists. It is whether the pattern of testimony — its structural properties, its specific descriptors, its distribution across time and geography — can be fully accounted for by conventional explanations: misidentification of known animals, deliberate fabrication, or social contagion through shared media. We argue that the data presents empirical challenges that these explanations do not fully resolve, and that the phenomenon warrants serious scientific attention on those grounds alone.

The methodology itself constitutes an independent contribution. We present a replicable pipeline — combining automated transcription, structured AI-assisted extraction, and multi-dimensional credibility scoring — that enables a single researcher to process hundreds of hours of testimony in days rather than years. We document this pipeline in full so that future researchers may apply it to other testimony corpora, refine its scoring dimensions, or challenge our interpretations.

2. Prior Work and Theoretical Framework

2.1 Forensic credibility analysis

The assessment of witness testimony credibility has a substantial literature in forensic psychology. Criteria-Based Content Analysis (CBCA), originally developed by Steller and Köhnken (1989) for the evaluation of child sexual abuse testimony, identifies structural markers that distinguish genuine experiential memory from fabricated accounts. These include logical consistency, unstructured production, quantity of detail, contextual embedding, and accounts of subjective mental states. The validity of CBCA as a credibility indicator has been supported across multiple meta-analyses (Vrij, 2005; Masip et al., 2005).

The Statement Validity Assessment (SVA) framework, of which CBCA is a component, has been applied in criminal proceedings across Europe and North America. While its use in forensic contexts remains debated, its core insight — that genuine traumatic memory has structural properties that differ from fabricated narrative — is well supported empirically. We adapt these principles into our six-dimension WCS framework, calibrated for the specific properties of anomalous encounter testimony.

2.2 Memory and trauma

Research on traumatic memory encoding is relevant to our methodology in two respects. First, genuinely traumatic experiences tend to produce memories with high sensory specificity and emotional vividness, often accompanied by physiological responses during recall (van der Kolk, 1994). Second, the phenomenon of emotional flattening during recall — where a witness describes extreme events in a calm, analytical tone — is itself consistent with dissociative trauma processing rather than fabrication, provided that sensory specificity remains high (Brewin et al., 1996). Our WCS treats emotional authenticity and sensory specificity as independent dimensions precisely because their relationship in genuine trauma is non-trivial.

2.3 The problem of social contagion

A primary alternative explanation for convergent anomalous testimony is social contagion: the spread of descriptors through shared media, creating apparent pattern where none exists in underlying experience. This explanation predicts that unusual descriptors should increase in frequency following high-visibility media events, and that pre-internet accounts should show lower rates of specific unusual descriptors than post-internet accounts. We test this prediction directly using the date metadata available for accounts in our corpus.

2.4 Historical and cross-cultural parallels

Bipedal canine-headed humanoids appear across cultures and centuries in forms that predate any plausible transmission route. The cynocephali of classical antiquity (Ctesias, 4th century BCE; Pliny the Elder, 77 CE) describe dog-headed beings in geographically distant territories. Medieval European manuscript traditions depict them as a distinct species. Slavic folklore preserves the psoglav (Serbian) and pasjeglavci (Slovenian) — canine-headed humanoids with specific behavioural and anatomical properties, some of which — including anatomically specific gaze limitations — have no parallel in the classical tradition and therefore resist explanation as classical borrowing. We discuss these parallels in relation to our corpus findings in Section 6.

3. The Corpus: Dogman Encounters Archive

3.1 Source description

The primary corpus for this study is the Dogman Encounters podcast, hosted by Vic Cundiff and in continuous production since 2014. At the time of data collection, the archive comprised over 600 episodes. Each episode consists of one or more long-form witness interviews, typically 30–90 minutes in duration, conducted in a consistent format by a single interviewer. This consistency is methodologically significant: unlike corpora assembled from heterogeneous sources, the uniform interview conditions reduce confounds introduced by varying interviewer style, question format, and session length.

3.2 Witness recruitment and vetting

Witnesses are not self-selected in the manner typical of online anomalous experience platforms. The majority are referred to the programme through personal networks, often by individuals who themselves had prior contact with the host. Referrals from professional sources — including park rangers and law enforcement personnel — are documented across multiple episodes. These professionals carry institutional credibility and professional reputational stakes, making their referrals an implicit endorsement of the witness's character.

The host applies a vetting process prior to invitation. Witnesses whose accounts contain demonstrable inconsistencies or implausibilities are not invited to record. This pre-screening layer means our corpus has already passed a human credibility filter before our analytical scoring is applied — a fact we note as both a strength (reduced noise floor) and a limitation (potential interviewer selection bias) of the dataset.

3.3 Social dynamics and disclosure

The platform's audience is characterised by experiential engagement rather than adversarial scepticism. Listener responses are predominantly empathetic, with a high proportion describing analogous personal encounters or knowledge of individuals who experienced them. This social environment is notable for what it is not: unlike most paranormal media platforms, the comment section is not characterised by the mockery and adversarial engagement typical of evidence-based cryptid content. An outside observer encountering the platform for the first time consistently reports surprise at the quality of engagement — expecting ridicule, finding instead a community organised around shared experience rather than shared belief in a specific extraordinary claim.

This dynamic has implications for the embellishment hypothesis. Embellishment is motivated by the need to convince a sceptical audience. In an environment where the audience is already sympathetic, the pressure to dramatise is reduced, not increased. We would therefore expect this corpus to show less embellishment than testimony recorded in adversarial conditions, not more.

Multiple witnesses report that the act of public disclosure produced a significant reduction in psychological distress — a sense of relief at finally being believed. This pattern is consistent with the established psychology of trauma disclosure (Pennebaker, 1997), in which verbalisation of suppressed traumatic experience produces measurable affective relief. Fabricators do not experience this relief because there is no suppressed experience to process. The consistent report of peace following disclosure is structurally consistent with genuine traumatic experience.

3.4 The historical window

It is worth noting explicitly that this study could not have been conducted at this scale at any prior point in history. The combination of long-form podcast archives, automated speech-to-text transcription, and large language model extraction and analysis creates analytical possibilities that did not exist even a decade ago. The Dogman Encounters archive represents the first testimony corpus of sufficient scale, consistency, and accessibility to support the methodology we describe here. We are, in a meaningful sense, working in a window that has only recently opened.

4. Methodology

4.1 Pipeline overview

Our analytical pipeline consists of five stages: audio acquisition, transcription, structured extraction, credibility scoring, and statistical analysis. All pipeline code and prompts are documented in the project repository and are fully replicable.

4.2 Transcription

Audio files are transcribed using OpenAI's Whisper large-v3 model via the Groq inference API, producing word-level timestamped transcripts. Mean transcription time is approximately one minute per hour of audio. All transcripts are stored as structured JSON including segment-level timestamps, enabling downstream acoustic analysis.

4.3 Structured extraction

Each transcript is processed by a large language model (Anthropic Claude) using a structured extraction prompt that elicits approximately 80 fields per account. These fields cover: witness background and professional context; encounter geography, timing, and conditions; creature morphology and behaviour; witness physiological and psychological responses; post-encounter sequelae; testimony structure markers; light anomalies; and anomaly assessment. Extraction confidence is reported per field. Fields not mentioned in the transcript are returned as null rather than inferred.

4.4 Witness Credibility Score (WCS)

Each account is scored on six dimensions adapted from CBCA and SVA frameworks:

DimensionAbbr.WeightDescription
Internal ConsistencyIC20%Account stability; absence of self-contradiction
Sensory SpecificitySS20%Multi-channel sensory detail; peripheral context
Spontaneous ElaborationSA15%Unprompted detail; self-correction; non-linear structure
Emotional AuthenticityEA25%Appropriate affect; disclosure delay; post-trauma markers
Contextual PlausibilityCP10%Geographic, temporal, and social context coherence
CorroborationCO10%Independent witnesses; physical evidence; prior reports

Each dimension is scored 0–10 by the language model, with rationale and evidence quotes required for each score. The weighted composite WCS is classified into five bands: Exceptional (≥8.5), Strong (≥7.0), Moderate (≥5.5), Weak (≥4.0), and Unreliable (<4.0). Emotional Authenticity is weighted highest (25%) as it is the dimension most resistant to deliberate manipulation — performed emotion has distinct structural properties from genuine affect.

4.5 Acoustic analysis

In parallel with transcript-based scoring, each audio file is analysed using the librosa audio processing library to extract prosodic features at the segment level. Measures include fundamental frequency (pitch), RMS energy (volume proxy), speaking rate, pause duration, and pitch tremor (instability index). Segments showing statistically significant deviation from the speaker's baseline on any of these measures are flagged with an emotional weight score. This acoustic layer provides an independent, non-linguistic signal of emotional response — one that cannot be manipulated through word choice alone — and enables identification of the specific narrative moments at which physiological emotional response occurred.

4.6 Descriptor frequency analysis

Binary flags for specific anatomical and behavioural descriptors are extracted for each account and aggregated across the corpus. We test three hypotheses for each descriptor: (1) its frequency exceeds base rate expectation for random embellishment; (2) its frequency is stable across accounts predating 2010 versus post-2010 accounts; (3) its frequency is equal or higher in high-WCS accounts compared to low-WCS accounts. A descriptor meeting all three criteria is classified as a Primary Finding.

6. Discussion

6.1 Against the fabrication hypothesis

The fabrication hypothesis predicts that unusual descriptors should cluster in low-credibility accounts — that embellishment and creativity correlate with implausibility. Our data [will test / tests] this prediction directly. If high-WCS accounts report unusual descriptors at equal or higher rates than low-WCS accounts, the fabrication hypothesis cannot account for the pattern. A skilled fabricator produces structurally clean narrative; a genuine experiencer produces structurally authentic narrative with unusual content. The WCS is specifically designed to separate these profiles.

We also note the disclosure cost argument. Many witnesses in this corpus held their accounts for years or decades before public disclosure — in some cases explicitly citing fear of professional consequences. Police officers, military personnel, nurses, teachers, and park rangers appear throughout the corpus. The expected cost of fabrication for these individuals substantially exceeds any plausible benefit. This does not prove their accounts are genuine, but it is inconsistent with the motivated fabrication profile.

6.2 Against the social contagion hypothesis

Social contagion predicts temporal instability in descriptor rates — unusual details should become more common as media coverage increases. We test this by comparing accounts from before and after the widespread popularisation of dogman content online (approximately 2012–2015). Stable rates across this boundary are inconsistent with contagion as the primary mechanism.

We further note that the platform's social environment — characterised by belief rather than scepticism — reduces rather than increases the incentive for embellishment. Witnesses are not trying to convince a hostile audience. The pressure to dramatise is structurally lower than in adversarial disclosure contexts.

6.3 Cross-cultural and historical convergence

The appearance of specific morphological descriptors in geographically and temporally isolated historical sources — including the anatomically specific downward gaze limitation documented in Slovenian pasjeglavci folklore (oči obrnjene v tla: "eyes turned toward the ground") which has no parallel in the classical cynocephali tradition — raises the possibility of independent convergence on a real perceptual object. We do not assert this conclusion, but we note that independent convergence without transmission route is itself a phenomenon requiring explanation.

6.4 Limitations

Several limitations of this study require acknowledgment. First, the corpus is pre-screened by the host prior to recording, introducing a selection bias that may inflate credibility scores relative to an unscreened population of experiencers. Second, AI-based credibility scoring has not been independently validated against human forensic expert ratings for this specific testimony type; our WCS dimensions are derived from validated frameworks but the specific implementation is novel. Third, acoustic analysis of emotional response cannot distinguish genuine involuntary physiological response from skilled performance — though we note that sustained performance across 90-minute unedited interviews represents an implausibly high level of acting skill for non-professional witnesses. Fourth, our corpus is drawn from a single platform with a single consistent interviewer, limiting generalisability to other encounter report archives.

7. Conclusion

After analysing [N] accounts using forensic credibility tools developed for criminal testimony, we find that [X]% score at or above the threshold associated with genuine traumatic recall. These accounts share specific anatomical and behavioural descriptors at statistically significant rates — descriptors that appear consistently across decades and geographic regions, and that predate widespread internet discussion of the phenomenon.

Two explanations remain. Either this corpus represents an extraordinary concentration of skilled, coordinated fabricators — people with nothing to gain and often significant professional costs to bear — producing structurally authentic accounts that independently converge on the same unusual details across five decades. Or the descriptors reflect genuine perceptual experience of something not yet catalogued by mainstream science.

We make no claim as to which explanation is correct. We note only that the data presents a problem for both — and that a phenomenon capable of generating this quality and consistency of testimony, whatever its ultimate nature, warrants serious scientific attention.

The pipeline we have documented here — the first of its kind applied to anomalous encounter testimony at this scale — is offered to the research community as a replicable tool. We invite challenge, replication, and refinement. The data is the argument. We are simply the first to read it this carefully.
