Persistent Descriptors in Anomalous Encounter Testimony
A forensic credibility analysis of the Dogman Encounter corpus — the first systematic application of structured witness assessment to large-scale anomalous interview archives
Kryptovox Research Project Primary corpus: Dogman Encounters (Vic Cundiff, 2014–present) Pipeline v1.0 Status: Data collection in progress
Abstract

This paper presents the first systematic forensic credibility analysis of anomalous bipedal encounter testimony at corpus scale. Drawing on a primary corpus of [N] witness interviews from the Dogman Encounters podcast archive, we apply a six-dimension Witness Credibility Score (WCS) derived from established forensic psychology tools to assess the structural authenticity of individual accounts. We further conduct frequency analysis of specific anatomical and behavioural descriptors across the corpus, testing whether unusual details cluster in high-credibility accounts and whether their prevalence is stable across accounts predating widespread internet discussion of the phenomenon. We report [X]% of accounts scoring at or above the Strong credibility threshold (WCS ≥ 7.0), and identify [Y] descriptors appearing at statistically significant rates among high-credibility accounts. We discuss these findings in relation to conventional explanations — misidentification, fabrication, and social contagion — and conclude that the data presents empirical challenges that neither explanation fully addresses. We make no claim regarding the ontological status of the phenomenon described.

1 — Introduction

Across North America, thousands of individuals report encounters with a large bipedal creature bearing canine facial features. These accounts are not new — analogous descriptions appear in indigenous oral tradition, colonial-era settler records, and the folklore of culturally isolated populations across multiple continents. What is new is the scale and accessibility of the testimony archive available for analysis.

Prior to the emergence of long-form podcast platforms, no comparable body of primary anomalous encounter testimony existed in accessible form. The Dogman Encounters archive alone represents over 600 unedited witness interviews conducted by a single interviewer over more than a decade — a dataset of a consistency and scale that was simply unavailable to researchers even fifteen years ago. This study represents the first attempt to apply systematic forensic credibility analysis to this archive at corpus scale.

The central question we address is not whether the described creature exists. It is whether the pattern of testimony can be fully accounted for by conventional explanations.

We argue that the data presents empirical challenges that these explanations do not fully resolve, and that the phenomenon warrants serious scientific attention on those grounds alone.

The methodology itself constitutes an independent contribution. We present a replicable pipeline — combining automated transcription, structured AI-assisted extraction, and multi-dimensional credibility scoring — that enables a single researcher to process hundreds of hours of testimony in days rather than years. We document this pipeline in full so that future researchers may apply it to other testimony corpora, refine its scoring dimensions, or challenge our interpretations.

2 — Prior Work and Theoretical Framework
2.1 Forensic credibility analysis

The assessment of witness testimony credibility has a substantial literature in forensic psychology. Criteria-Based Content Analysis (CBCA), originally developed by Steller and Köhnken (1989) for the evaluation of child sexual abuse testimony, identifies structural markers that distinguish genuine experiential memory from fabricated accounts. These include logical consistency, unstructured production, quantity of detail, contextual embedding, and accounts of subjective mental states. The validity of CBCA as a credibility indicator has been supported across multiple meta-analyses (Vrij, 2005; Masip et al., 2005).

We adapt these principles into our six-dimension WCS framework, calibrated for the specific properties of anomalous encounter testimony.

2.2 Memory and trauma

Research on traumatic memory encoding is relevant to our methodology in two respects. First, genuinely traumatic experiences tend to produce memories with high sensory specificity and emotional vividness, often accompanied by physiological responses during recall (van der Kolk, 1994). Second, the phenomenon of emotional flattening during recall — where a witness describes extreme events in a calm, analytical tone — is itself consistent with dissociative trauma processing rather than fabrication, provided that sensory specificity remains high (Brewin et al., 1996).

2.3 The problem of social contagion

A primary alternative explanation for convergent anomalous testimony is social contagion: the spread of descriptors through shared media, creating apparent pattern where none exists in underlying experience. This explanation predicts that unusual descriptors should increase in frequency following high-visibility media events, and that pre-internet accounts should show lower rates of specific unusual descriptors than post-internet accounts. We test this prediction directly using the date metadata available for accounts in our corpus.

2.4 Historical and cross-cultural parallels

Bipedal canine-headed humanoids appear across cultures and centuries in forms that predate any plausible transmission route. The cynocephali of classical antiquity, Slavic psoglav and pasjeglavci, Mayan Huay Chivo, Navajo skin-walker traditions, and Siberian shamanic encounter accounts each describe beings with overlapping morphological and — crucially — behavioural properties.

Of particular note is the Slovenian pasjeglavci detail of oči obrnjene v tla — eyes turned toward the ground, anatomically incapable of looking upward — which appears in no other tradition worldwide and has no plausible transmission route to modern American witnesses. We discuss the cross-cultural record in full in Section 6.

3 — The Corpus: Dogman Encounters Archive
3.1 Source description

The primary corpus is the Dogman Encounters podcast, hosted by Vic Cundiff and in continuous production since 2014. At the time of data collection, the archive comprised over 600 episodes. Each episode consists of one or more long-form witness interviews, typically 30–90 minutes in duration, conducted in a consistent format by a single interviewer. This consistency is methodologically significant: unlike corpora assembled from heterogeneous sources, the uniform interview conditions reduce confounds introduced by varying interviewer style, question format, and session length.

3.2 Witness recruitment and vetting

Witnesses are not self-selected in the manner typical of online anomalous experience platforms. The majority are referred through personal networks — often by individuals who themselves had prior contact with the host. Referrals from professional sources, including park rangers and law enforcement personnel, are documented across multiple episodes. The host applies a vetting process prior to invitation: witnesses whose accounts contain demonstrable inconsistencies are not invited to record.

3.3 Social dynamics and disclosure

The platform's audience is characterised by experiential engagement rather than adversarial scepticism. An outside observer encountering the platform for the first time consistently reports surprise at the quality of engagement — expecting ridicule, finding instead a community organised around shared experience rather than shared belief in a specific extraordinary claim.

In an environment where the audience is already sympathetic, the pressure to dramatise is reduced, not increased. We would expect this corpus to show less embellishment than testimony recorded in adversarial conditions.

Multiple witnesses report that the act of public disclosure produced a significant reduction in psychological distress — a sense of relief at finally being believed. This pattern is consistent with the established psychology of trauma disclosure (Pennebaker, 1997), in which verbalisation of suppressed traumatic experience produces measurable affective relief. Fabricators do not experience this relief because there is no suppressed experience to process.

3.4 The historical window

This study could not have been conducted at this scale at any prior point in history. The combination of long-form podcast archives, automated speech-to-text transcription, and large language model extraction and analysis creates analytical possibilities that did not exist even a decade ago. We are working in a window that has only recently opened — and the Dogman Encounters archive is the first testimony corpus of sufficient scale, consistency, and accessibility to support the methodology we describe here.

4 — Methodology
4.1 Pipeline overview

Our analytical pipeline consists of five stages: audio acquisition, transcription, structured extraction, credibility scoring, and statistical analysis. All pipeline code and prompts are documented in the project repository and are fully replicable.

4.2 Transcription

Audio files are transcribed using OpenAI's Whisper large-v3 model via the Groq inference API, producing segment-level timestamped transcripts. Mean transcription time is approximately one minute per hour of audio at approximately 100× realtime processing speed.

4.3 Structured extraction

Each transcript is processed by a large language model (Anthropic Claude Haiku) using a structured extraction prompt that elicits approximately 80 fields per account, covering: witness background and professional context; encounter geography, timing, and conditions; creature morphology and behaviour; witness physiological and psychological responses; post-encounter sequelae; testimony structure markers; light anomalies; community dynamics; and anomaly assessment.

4.4 Witness Credibility Score (WCS)

Accounts are scored on six dimensions adapted from CBCA and SVA forensic frameworks:

DimensionAbbr.WeightDescription
Internal ConsistencyIC20%Account stability; absence of self-contradiction
Sensory SpecificitySS20%Multi-channel sensory detail; peripheral context
Spontaneous ElaborationSA15%Unprompted detail; self-correction; non-linear structure
Emotional AuthenticityEA25%Appropriate affect; disclosure delay; post-trauma markers
Contextual PlausibilityCP10%Geographic, temporal, and social context coherence
CorroborationCO10%Independent witnesses; physical evidence; prior reports

Emotional Authenticity is weighted highest (25%) as it is the dimension most resistant to deliberate manipulation. The weighted composite WCS is classified into five bands: Exceptional (≥8.5), Strong (≥7.0), Moderate (≥5.5), Weak (≥4.0), and Unreliable (<4.0).

4.5 Acoustic analysis

In parallel with transcript-based scoring, each audio file is analysed using the librosa audio processing library to extract prosodic features at the segment level — fundamental frequency (pitch), RMS energy, speaking rate, pause duration, and pitch tremor. Segments showing statistically significant deviation from the speaker's baseline are flagged with an emotional weight score. This acoustic layer provides an independent, non-linguistic signal of emotional response that cannot be manipulated through word choice alone.

4.6 Descriptor frequency analysis

Binary flags for specific anatomical and behavioural descriptors are extracted for each account and aggregated across the corpus. We test three hypotheses for each descriptor: (1) its frequency exceeds base rate expectation for random embellishment; (2) its frequency is stable across accounts predating 2010 versus post-2010 accounts; (3) its frequency is equal or higher in high-WCS accounts compared to low-WCS accounts. A descriptor meeting all three criteria is classified as a Primary Finding.

6 — Discussion
6.1 Against the fabrication hypothesis

The fabrication hypothesis predicts that unusual descriptors should cluster in low-credibility accounts — that embellishment and creativity correlate with implausibility. If high-WCS accounts report unusual descriptors at equal or higher rates than low-WCS accounts, the fabrication hypothesis cannot account for the pattern. A skilled fabricator produces structurally clean narrative; a genuine experiencer produces structurally authentic narrative with unusual content. The WCS is specifically designed to separate these profiles.

We also note the disclosure cost argument. Many witnesses held their accounts for years or decades before public disclosure — in some cases explicitly citing fear of professional consequences. Police officers, military personnel, nurses, teachers, and park rangers appear throughout the corpus. The expected cost of fabrication for these individuals substantially exceeds any plausible benefit.

6.2 Against the social contagion hypothesis

Social contagion predicts temporal instability in descriptor rates. We test this by comparing accounts predating the widespread popularisation of dogman content online (approximately 2012–2015). Stable rates across this boundary are inconsistent with contagion as the primary mechanism. Furthermore, the platform's social environment — characterised by belief rather than scepticism — reduces rather than increases the incentive for embellishment.

6.3 Cross-cultural and historical convergence

Perhaps the most striking finding of this analysis is not any single descriptor frequency, but the pattern of convergence itself. Witnesses in rural America, predominantly with no academic background in folklore or anthropology, independently produce accounts that echo — in specific behavioural and morphological detail — traditions recorded in medieval Slovenia, pre-colonial Yucatan, Navajo oral history, and Siberian shamanic literature.

These witnesses are not describing a creature they read about. They are describing an experience. And that experience, filtered through entirely different cultures, languages, centuries, and continents, keeps producing the same details.

None of these witnesses cited Ctesias. None of them had read the Slovenian fairy tales collected by Bolhar in 1965. None of them were aware that a Mayan tradition described glowing eyes, or that a Navajo word exists for the specific paralysis they experienced. They just described what happened.

The appearance of the anatomically specific downward gaze limitation (oči obrnjene v tla) in Slovenian pasjeglavci tradition — in no other historical source worldwide — creates a precise test case for independent convergence. If this descriptor appears in modern corpus accounts with no plausible transmission route, it represents the strongest possible cross-cultural convergence evidence available to this study.

6.4 Community structure as independent evidence

The social structure of the Dogman Encounters community is itself anomalous relative to communities known to form around fabricated or misidentified phenomena. Characterised by peer referral recruitment, high disclosure delay, relief-dominant emotional response to sharing, and low monetisation of shared experience, it more closely resembles the structural signatures of stigmatised experience communities than entertainment or belief communities. This structural analysis provides an independent, non-testimony-based line of evidence that the accounts reflect genuine experience of something — whatever that something is.

6.5 Limitations

Several limitations require acknowledgment. The corpus is pre-screened by the host, introducing selection bias. AI-based credibility scoring has not been independently validated against human forensic expert ratings for this specific testimony type. Acoustic analysis cannot distinguish genuine involuntary physiological response from skilled performance, though sustained performance across unedited 90-minute interviews represents an implausibly high bar for non-professional witnesses. The corpus is drawn from a single platform with a single consistent interviewer, limiting generalisability.

7 — Conclusion

After analysing [N] accounts using forensic credibility tools developed for criminal testimony, we find that [X]% score at or above the threshold associated with genuine traumatic recall. These accounts share specific anatomical and behavioural descriptors at statistically significant rates — descriptors that appear consistently across decades and geographic regions, and that predate widespread internet discussion of the phenomenon.

Two explanations remain. Either this corpus represents an extraordinary concentration of skilled, coordinated fabricators — people with nothing to gain and often significant professional costs to bear — producing structurally authentic accounts that independently converge on the same unusual details across five decades. Or the descriptors reflect genuine perceptual experience of something not yet catalogued by mainstream science.

We make no claim as to which explanation is correct. We note only that the data presents a problem for both — and that a phenomenon capable of generating this quality and consistency of testimony warrants serious scientific attention.

What has happened quietly, in forests and mountains, on roads at night, at the edges of human settlement — everywhere, throughout recorded history, and almost certainly long before — has now left a record large enough to read. We are simply the first to read it this carefully.
