The analytical framework

We treat witness testimony the same way a forensic psychologist treats criminal evidence.

Thousands of people have described encountering a large, bipedal, dog-headed being — in forests, at night, often alone. They describe paralysis. They describe it watching them. They describe not telling anyone for years. Most have nothing to gain from disclosure. Many describe losing things by coming forward at all.

The standard response is dismissal. Ours is measurement. We apply the same tools used to evaluate criminal witness testimony — structured extraction, multi-dimensional credibility scoring, and acoustic emotion analysis — to build the first scientifically rigorous corpus of anomalous encounter testimony at scale.

We make no claim about what witnesses saw. We claim only that the testimony, analysed systematically, presents a pattern that neither fabrication nor social contagion fully explains.

The scoring system

Witness Credibility Score — six dimensions

Derived from CBCA (Criteria-Based Content Analysis) and SVA (Statement Validity Assessment) — forensic frameworks developed to distinguish genuine traumatic memory from fabrication in criminal proceedings. Each dimension scored 0–10, weighted by how feasible it is to fake and how diagnostic it is for this corpus.

EA — Emotional Authenticity
25%
Does the emotional affect match the content? Are distress markers present at morphological description moments specifically? Does the witness avoid over-performing emotional credibility?

Highest weight: hardest to fake consistently across an hour-long interview.
SD — Sensory Detail
20%
Multi-channel sensory specificity — sight, sound, smell, temperature, tactile experience. Does the witness describe peripheral sensory context (what they were doing, ambient conditions) rather than just the creature?

Genuine memories embed in context. Fabricated accounts tend to front-load the "interesting" content.
IC — Internal Consistency
20%
Does the account contradict itself? Are physical details consistent with stated environment and witness position? Does the narrative timeline hold together?

Also flags format — documentary format (IC=1) is detected here and filters the account to Unreliable.
DC — Disclosure Cost
20%
Did coming forward cost the witness something? Career risk, relationship impact, ridicule? The higher the cost, the less consistent with fabrication.

Key sub-fields: career_risk_score (0–3), relationship_impact, hesitation_to_disclose, hesitation_description.
NC — Narrative Coherence
15%
Is the overall account structure coherent? Does it move plausibly through context → encounter → response → aftermath? Are there spontaneous corrections and self-doubt?

Self-correction is a positive credibility marker, not a negative one.
CO — Corroboration
10%
Physical evidence, additional witnesses, environmental confirmation. Rare in this corpus by the nature of the phenomenon — hence lowest weight. Present when available (multiple witnesses, ground trace, animal reactions).

Absence of corroboration is not a negative marker in this corpus; its presence is a strong positive one.

Stage 3 in depth
What we extract from every account
80+ structured fields. Every account. The same schema. No editorial discretion between transcription and extraction.

The extraction stage transforms a 60–90 minute spoken interview into a structured data record. The prompt was developed iteratively over the pilot corpus and is frozen for the main run to ensure comparability across all accounts.

Creature morphology

  • height_estimatestring — e.g. "7–8 feet"
  • buildstring — muscular / lean / broad / unknown
  • head_typestring — canine / feline / ursine / indeterminate
  • eye_descriptionstring — colour, luminescence, position
  • self_illuminated_eyesboolean
  • locomotionstring — bipedal / quadrupedal / switches
  • fur_descriptionstring — colour, length, texture
  • vocalisationstring — growl, howl, bark, silent, human-like

Encounter behaviour

  • watches_then_leavesboolean — key cross-cultural match
  • silent_departureboolean — no sound on exit
  • felt_known_by_creatureboolean — witness felt specifically targeted
  • aggression_demonstratedboolean
  • proximity_minimumstring — estimated closest distance
  • duration_estimatestring
  • encounter_environmentstring — forest, rural road, field, suburban

Witness physiology at encounter

  • paralysis_or_freezeboolean — involuntary immobility
  • infrasound_feltboolean — chest vibration, felt not heard
  • tunnel_visionboolean
  • time_distortionboolean — subjective time passing differently
  • smell_describedboolean
  • smell_descriptionstring
  • pre_encounter_silenceboolean — animals/birds go silent before

Community structure

  • referral_sourcestring — how they found the podcast
  • referral_typeenum — peer / search / prior_disclosure / unknown
  • hesitation_to_discloseboolean
  • disclosure_delay_yearsnumber — years between encounter and disclosure
  • relationship_impactboolean — disclosure affected relationships
  • career_risk_scoreinteger 0–3 — professional cost
  • sought_explanation_before_disclosingboolean
  • witness_demeanourstring — reluctant, matter-of-fact, distressed, etc.

Schema freeze: The extraction schema is frozen for the main 100-account run. No new fields will be added until after the 100-account milestone review — at which point a single schema update will be applied and all 100 accounts re-extracted with the final schema before Phase 2 scaling.

Stage 5 in depth
Reading the voice beneath the words
Acoustic analysis adds an independent channel. When text and voice agree on emotional state, credibility confidence increases substantially.

A person can rehearse words. It is much harder to rehearse the exact moment and magnitude of vocal tremor. The acoustic analysis layer extracts prosodic features — measurable properties of the voice signal — and aligns them with transcript segments to produce an emotional timeline per account.

Features extracted per segment

  • pitch_mean / pitch_stdHz — F0, fundamental frequency
  • rms_energyamplitude RMS — volume
  • speaking_ratewords per second
  • pause_countnumber of distinct pauses ≥0.3s
  • spectral_centroidbrightness — rises under stress
  • zero_crossing_ratevoice roughness / breathiness

Emotional flags derived

  • voice_tremorpitch_std > 1.5× corpus mean
  • pitch_dropsudden F0 decrease — awe, fear, resignation
  • pitch_risesudden F0 increase — urgency, surprise
  • speech_slowdownrate < 0.6× segment mean — effortful recall
  • long_pausepause ≥2.5s — emotional processing
  • volume_dropRMS < 0.5× mean — intimacy, fear, disclosure cost

The key finding from pilot analysis: vocal stress peaks cluster on specific morphological description moments. When a witness says "it had a dog's head," "it was standing on two legs," or "the eyes were — I've never seen anything like the eyes" — those segments show elevated stress markers at statistically significant rates compared to narrative connective segments.

This pattern is what forensic psychologists call affect–content congruence. It is not what you would expect if the emotional response were performed for an audience.

What the data shows
Why neither explanation holds
We test two competing explanations. Both fail in specific, measurable ways.

Explanation 1: Mass fabrication

If accounts are fabricated, we would expect:

— Low WCS scores (emotional incoherence, sensory vagueness, narrative inconsistency)
— Uniform descriptors driven by shared "dogman" mythology
— Low disclosure cost — fabricators don't suffer for their stories
— Vocal stress distributed evenly rather than spiking on morphological moments
— Short or absent disclosure delay

We observe: 60% of accounts scoring Strong or higher on WCS. High and consistent disclosure costs including career risk and relationship damage. Disclosure delays averaging years to decades. Acoustic stress peaks on specific morphological moments. The fabrication hypothesis does not explain this pattern.

Explanation 2: Social contagion

If descriptors are spreading through online exposure to dogman content, we would expect:

— Unusual descriptors becoming more frequent after 2015 (peak Dogman Encounters exposure)
— Descriptors concentrating in younger witnesses with more internet access
— Correlation between specific unusual descriptors and access to the same sources
— Absence of unusual descriptors in accounts from before the podcast era

We observe: watches-then-leaves, silent departure, and paralysis appear at consistent rates in pre-2010 and post-2015 accounts. The same descriptors appear in Slovenian folklore, Navajo tradition, and Mayan oral history — sources with no plausible transmission route to contemporary American witnesses. Social contagion does not explain this pattern.

What we conclude

We do not conclude that a dogman exists. We conclude that the testimony, evaluated on its own terms using rigorous forensic methodology, presents a pattern that the two most common dismissive explanations cannot account for.

The question this leaves open is the interesting one.
